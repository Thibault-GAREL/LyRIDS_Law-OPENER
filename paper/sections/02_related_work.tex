\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Open-world and zero-shot NER}
Open-world NER recognises entity types not fixed at training time. GLiNER \cite{zaratiana2024gliner} scores spans against candidate type names with a bidirectional encoder; GNER \cite{ding2024gner} casts NER as generation; OWNER \cite{genest2025owner} trains a dedicated entity encoder and clusters mentions into dynamically named types. Zero-shot typing via type-name semantics has also been studied \cite{aly2021zeroshot}. OPENER differs by reusing a pretrained embedding rather than training an encoder; here we ask how that reuse transfers to the legal domain.

\subsection{Entity representations and contrastive learning}
OPENER builds on pretrained sentence embedders \cite{nussbaum2024nomic, reimers2019sbert} with Matryoshka truncation \cite{kusupati2022matryoshka}, sharpened by a triplet objective \cite{schroff2015facenet}; the typing heads use a balanced linear classifier \cite{fan2008liblinear} and AMI \cite{vinh2010ami} as the primary metric.

\subsection{Legal NLP and legal NER}
Legal text combines a specialised vocabulary, long formulaic documents and entity inventories specific to each jurisdiction and document type, which has motivated dedicated resources and models. On the modelling side, domain-adapted language models such as Legal-BERT \citep{chalkidis2020legalbert} and multi-task benchmarks such as LexGLUE \citep{chalkidis2022lexglue} show that in-domain pretraining and evaluation matter for legal NLU. On the NER side, several annotated corpora expose schema-specific types: E-NER \citep{au2022ener} annotates U.S.\ SEC/EDGAR filings with seven classes including legal-specific ones (court, government body, legislation/act), LeNER-Br \citep{luzdearaujo2018lenerbr} covers Brazilian court decisions with dedicated \emph{legislation} and \emph{case-law} tags, and German-LER \citep{leitner2020germanler} annotates German federal decisions with a fine-grained 19-class legal schema. Crucially, \citet{au2022ener} report that NER models trained on general English degrade sharply when tested on legal text, which is precisely the distribution shift we study: rather than retraining a legal-specific tagger, we ask how far a frozen, general-domain open-world pipeline transfers to these schemas, and we position it against a general-domain open-world detector and -- where applicable -- domain baselines (\autoref{sec:experiments}).

\subsection{Energy-aware evaluation}
Reporting efficiency alongside accuracy is increasingly advocated \cite{schwartz2020greenai, strubell2019energy, henderson2020systematic}; we adopt the three-axis (quality/latency/energy) view of the general-domain system, measured with CodeCarbon \cite{lacoste2019codecarbon}.
