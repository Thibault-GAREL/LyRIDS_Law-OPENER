\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Open-world and zero-shot NER}
Open-world NER recognises entity types not fixed at training time. GLiNER \citep{zaratiana2024gliner} scores spans against candidate type names with a bidirectional encoder, a DeBERTaV3 backbone \citep{he2023debertav3}; GNER \citep{ding2024gner} casts NER as generation with a sequence-to-sequence model \citep{raffel2020t5}; OWNER \citep{genest2025owner} trains a dedicated entity encoder and clusters mentions into dynamically named types. Zero-shot typing via type-name semantics has also been studied \citep{aly2021zeroshot}. A complementary line distils or prompts billion-parameter language models directly for open NER \citep{zhou2024universalner, sainz2024gollie, wei2024chatie}, at a compute and energy cost our comparison also probes via a small 4-bit LLM baseline (\autoref{sec:exp:setup}). OPENER differs from all of these by reusing a pretrained embedding rather than training an encoder; here we ask how that reuse transfers to the legal domain.

\subsection{Entity representations and contrastive learning}
OPENER builds on pretrained sentence embedders \citep{nussbaum2024nomic, reimers2019sbert} with Matryoshka truncation \citep{kusupati2022matryoshka}, sharpened by a triplet objective \citep{schroff2015facenet} on general-domain CoNLL-2003 spans \citep{tjongkimsang2003conll}; the typing heads use a balanced linear classifier \citep{fan2008liblinear} and AMI \citep{vinh2010ami} as the primary metric.

\subsection{Legal NLP and legal NER}
Legal NLP is an active field spanning summarisation, judgement prediction, question answering and information extraction \citep{ariai2025legalnlp}, driven by the specialised vocabulary, long formulaic documents and jurisdiction-specific entity inventories of legal text. On the modelling side, domain-adapted language models such as Legal-BERT \citep{chalkidis2020legalbert} and multi-task benchmarks such as LexGLUE \citep{chalkidis2022lexglue} show that in-domain pretraining and evaluation matter for legal NLU.

The dominant approach to legal NER is to \emph{train or adapt a supervised tagger for each corpus, schema and language}. LegNER \citep{karamitsos2025legner} domain-adapts BERT with span supervision for English legal NER and anonymisation; \citet{kalusev2025serbian} fine-tune a BERT-type model for Serbian court rulings together with a new dataset; and the LegalEval shared task (SemEval-2023 Task~6) spurred systems such as a Bi-LSTM with Flair embeddings for Indian court judgements \citep{ramesh2023flairnlp}. Even LLM-based studies remain schema-bound: \citet{lu2025financial} prompt GPT-4o, LLaMA-3.1 and Gemini-1.5 for financial NER and report systematic failure modes on domain entities. These systems achieve strong in-corpus scores but each requires labelled data and (re)training tied to one schema and one language. The underlying corpora expose exactly such schema-specific types: E-NER \citep{au2022ener} annotates U.S.\ SEC/EDGAR filings with seven classes including legal-specific ones (court, government body, legislation/act), LeNER-Br \citep{luzdearaujo2018lenerbr} covers Brazilian court decisions with dedicated \emph{legislation} and \emph{case-law} tags, and German-LER \citep{leitner2020germanler} annotates German federal decisions with a fine-grained 19-class schema.

Crucially, \citet{au2022ener} report that NER models trained on general English degrade sharply on legal text -- precisely the distribution shift we study. Rather than retraining a legal-specific tagger per corpus and language, we ask how far a frozen, general-domain open-world pipeline transfers to these schemas, and we position it against a general-domain open-world detector and, where applicable, domain baselines (\autoref{sec:experiments}).

\subsection{Energy-aware evaluation}
Reporting efficiency alongside accuracy is increasingly advocated \citep{schwartz2020greenai, strubell2019energy, henderson2020systematic}, from the carbon cost of training \citep{patterson2022carbon} to a whole-lifecycle view of AI's environmental footprint \citep{wu2022sustainable} and to inference-time cost specifically, where general-purpose generative models are shown to be orders of magnitude more expensive per query than task-specific ones \citep{luccioni2024power} -- precisely the gap this frugal pipeline targets. We adopt the same three-axis (quality/latency/energy) view, measured with CodeCarbon \citep{lacoste2019codecarbon}.
