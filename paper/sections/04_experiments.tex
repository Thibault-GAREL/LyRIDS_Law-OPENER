\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Datasets}
\label{sec:exp:datasets}
We evaluate on four legal-specific NER sets across three languages that, together, separate two kinds of distribution shift. \emph{Two are in English}, isolating a \emph{pure domain shift} from the general-domain data the embedder was trained on: \textbf{E-NER} \citep{au2022ener} (annotated U.S.\ SEC/EDGAR filings, $7$ types) and \textbf{Indian-Legal} \citep{kalamkar2022indianlegal} (Indian court judgments, $14$ fine-grained legal types). \textbf{LeNER-Br} \citep{luzdearaujo2018lenerbr} (Brazilian court decisions, Portuguese) and \textbf{German-LER} \citep{leitner2020germanler} (German federal court decisions) add a \emph{cross-lingual} shift. All four annotate \emph{legal-specific} entity types (courts, statutes, legislation, case law, provisions), rather than the generic person/organisation/date schema of general NER. \autoref{tab:legal-datasets} summarises them with the entity-type distribution of their test mentions.

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:exp:setup}
Embedder, detector and heads are exactly those of the general-domain OPENER (\autoref{sec:method}); the entity embedder is loaded from the Hugging Face Hub and is \emph{not} retrained on legal data. Single fixed seed; each test split capped at $1000$ sentences. Since the reused embedder is frozen and no stage of the pipeline is stochastic at inference, the quality scores are deterministic (identical across re-runs and across seeds); following the general-domain protocol, we therefore quantify their sampling uncertainty with a non-parametric \textbf{bootstrap} ($K{=}1000$) rather than a multi-seed \emph{retraining} study. For typing-on-gold we resample the gold mentions (std reported inline in \autoref{tab:legal-gold}); for the end-to-end scores we resample the \emph{test sentences} and recompute the offset-aligned AMI \emph{paired} across systems, giving the significance test in \autoref{sec:exp:results}. All bootstraps are invariant across the resampling seeds $42$, $7$ and $123$ (CIs agree to ${\pm}0.1$ AMI).

For end-to-end runs the detector is a frozen, off-the-shelf GLiNER at threshold $0.3$, fed the target type names; since the detector is a modular, no-cost choice, each OPENER head uses the variant that maximises it (GLiNER-M for OPENER-Sup, whose probe is then fit on the detector's own train detections; GLiNER-L for OPENER-ZS). As \textbf{baselines} we evaluate the same open-world systems as the general-domain benchmark, applied to legal text: GLiNER-S/M/L \cite{zaratiana2024gliner}, the generative GNER-T5-base \cite{ding2024gner}, and a 4-bit Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct \cite{qwen2025} prompted for entities -- all zero-shot on the target type names. These are \emph{end-to-end} systems (they detect and type jointly), so they are compared only under the end-to-end protocol, never against typing-on-gold numbers. All quality figures are AMI ($\times 100$); German-LER uses its $7$-type coarse schema in the tables, and its $19$-type fine schema as a stress test below.
We also report per-sentence latency (p50) and energy (CodeCarbon) on the same $6$\,GB GPU, which thermally throttles under sustained load; per-dataset efficiency cells therefore carry ${\approx}{\pm}15\%$ variance and are read as indicative. OWNER \cite{genest2025owner} runs in batch and exposes no per-sentence call; following the general-domain protocol we therefore report an \emph{amortised} cost from its per-dataset wall-clock time $t$ (model load $+$ mention encoding $+$ clustering): energy $= t\times110$\,W (TDP estimate) and latency $= t/\text{sentences}$.

\subsection{Results}
\label{sec:exp:results}
Keeping the two protocols apart, \autoref{tab:legal-gold} reports typing-on-gold AMI (OPENER heads, perfect detection) and \autoref{tab:legal-main} the end-to-end comparison against the baselines; \autoref{tab:legal-latency} and \autoref{tab:legal-energy} give the accompanying per-dataset latency and energy. Three findings stand out.

\textbf{Supervised typing transfers well, including across languages.} Fitting a balanced linear probe on the frozen, general-domain embedder reaches $61.1$ mean typing-on-gold AMI over the four sets -- $70.0$ / $61.9$ on the two English corpora (E-NER, Indian court judgments), and still $53.3$ / $59.1$ on Portuguese LeNER-Br and German German-LER despite the English-centric embedder (\autoref{tab:legal-gold}). The probe adapts to whatever the embedder encodes, so target-label supervision largely absorbs the cross-lingual gap at typing time.

\textbf{Label-free typing is strong in English but degrades cross-lingually.} OPENER-ZS is usable on the two English sets -- $44.0$ (E-NER) and $46.3$ (Indian), from English label-name prototypes alone -- but drops to $31.6$ (pt) and $22.6$ (de): its English prototypes align well with English legal mentions, far less with other-language ones. The zero-shot head is where the cross-lingual distribution shift bites hardest.

\textbf{End-to-end, detection sets the ceiling.} On these legal-specific schemas every system clusters in a narrow $\approx 33$--$36$ AMI band (\autoref{tab:legal-main}), because the English detectors recover only a fraction of gold mentions (GLiNER-L offset recall $18.7$--$57.4\%$); with so few mentions detected, no typing head can pull far ahead, and OPENER-ZS's detector fusion essentially reverts to the GLiNER-L labels. Per-dataset winners are specific: OWNER \cite{genest2025owner} -- the closest prior work -- on E-NER ($38.4$) and GNER-T5 on German ($39.2$), though OWNER degrades most cross-lingually ($28.4$) and pays an order-of-magnitude higher inference cost (re-encoding and clustering every mention), while Qwen trails throughout ($23.1$ mean) and is the slowest. \emph{Averaged over the four sets, OPENER-Sup posts the best end-to-end AMI ($35.9$)} -- narrowly ahead of GLiNER-L ($35.0$) and GLiNER-M ($34.9$) -- and leads outright on Indian-Legal ($35.4$), while OPENER-ZS ($33.8$) essentially ties OWNER ($33.7$). The margins are small, as expected when detection is the ceiling, yet a \emph{paired} sentence-level bootstrap ($K{=}1000$ resamples of the test sentences) shows OPENER-Sup's end-to-end lead is nonetheless \textbf{statistically significant}: $+1.0$ AMI over GLiNER-M ($95\%$ CI $[+0.4,+1.7]$) and $+0.9$ over GLiNER-L ($[+0.1,+1.9]$), the lower bound staying above zero across resampling seeds $42/7/123$; OPENER-ZS's fusion, by contrast, sits significantly \emph{below} GLiNER-L ($-1.3$, $[-1.8,-0.8]$), as it largely defers to the detector's labels. Detection remains the ceiling, but OPENER's typing head is not the weak link -- consistent with the typing-on-gold results, where OPENER-Sup leads by a wide margin.

The three-axis picture (\autoref{tab:legal-latency}, \autoref{tab:legal-energy}) mirrors the general-domain study: OPENER runs a sentence end-to-end in $84$--$133$\,ms for $1.4$--$2.2$\,Wh -- on par with the GLiNER family, several times cheaper than OWNER's re-encode-and-cluster cost (${\approx}475$\,ms, $12$\,Wh amortised), and \emph{one to two orders of magnitude} below the generative GNER-T5 ($8.3$\,s, $79$\,Wh) and Qwen ($5.4$\,s, ${\approx}20$\,Wh). Zero-shot typing on gold is cheaper still ($0.5$\,Wh, $32$\,ms), as it embeds no training split. OPENER-Sup thus holds the best end-to-end accuracy at a small, fixed inference cost, which is the operating point that makes it deployable on modest hardware.

\subsection{Analysis}
\label{sec:exp:analysis}
Two distribution shifts act together here, and the results separate them cleanly. On \textbf{English} legal text (E-NER), where only the \emph{domain} shifts, transfer is strong: OPENER-Sup reaches $70.0$ gold AMI and OPENER-ZS a usable $44.0$ with no target labels at all, confirming that the general-domain contrastive embedder already places legal entity types (business, court, government body, legislation) in largely separable regions. Adding a \textbf{cross-lingual} shift (Portuguese, German) widens the gap between the two heads: the supervised probe stays high ($53.3$/$59.1$) because it re-fits on target labels, whereas the label-free head drops to $22$--$32$ because its English prototypes and the English embedder no longer match the target-language mentions.

The \textbf{specialised-schema} stress test points the same way. On German-LER's fine $19$-type schema, OPENER-Sup still reaches $62.0$ gold AMI -- the embedder separates even fine legal distinctions once a probe names them -- but OPENER-ZS falls to $22.1$, as many fine German type codes have no faithful English label-name prototype.

The binding constraint end-to-end is \textbf{detection}, not typing: GLiNER-L's offset recall falls from $57.4\%$ (en) to $18.7\%$ (de), so the end-to-end scores compress to a ${\approx}33$--$36$ band regardless of the typing head, within which OPENER-Sup nonetheless posts the best average ($35.9$) by re-typing the detected mentions with target-label supervision. This localises where domain adaptation would pay off first: a light, in-language adaptation of the \emph{detector} (or a multilingual one) would lift the ceiling far more than a better typing head. Within our frugal, retraining-free budget, OPENER-Sup is the safe operating point across languages, while OPENER-ZS is attractive only when the target language is close to the embedder's (English) training distribution.

\textbf{Fusion weight $\beta$.} OPENER-ZS's detector-fusion weight $\beta$ (fixed at $0.05$ throughout, inherited from the general-domain study and never tuned on the legal test sets) balances the label-name centroids against the detector's own zero-shot labels. Sweeping it post-hoc -- a pure inference hyperparameter, leaving the released embedder untouched -- tells the same story: at $\beta{=}0$ the head is a pure centroid classifier ($30.5$ average AMI), and as $\beta$ grows it interpolates smoothly up to the GLiNER-L detector's own labels ($35.0$), a plateau it already reaches by $\beta{\approx}0.2$. The label-free head can therefore do no better end-to-end than the detector it fuses with -- one more sign that detection, not typing, is the binding constraint.
