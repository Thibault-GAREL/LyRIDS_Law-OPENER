\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Datasets}
\label{sec:exp:datasets}
We evaluate on four legal-specific NER sets across three languages that, together, separate two kinds of distribution shift. \emph{Two are in English}, isolating a \emph{pure domain shift} from the general-domain data the embedder was trained on: E-NER \citep{au2022ener} (annotated U.S.\ SEC/EDGAR filings, $7$ types) and Indian-Legal \citep{kalamkar2022indianlegal} (Indian court judgments, $14$ fine-grained legal types). LeNER-Br \citep{luzdearaujo2018lenerbr} (Brazilian court decisions, Portuguese) and German-LER \citep{leitner2020germanler} (German federal court decisions) add a \emph{cross-lingual} shift. All four annotate \emph{legal-specific} entity types (courts, statutes, legislation, case law, provisions), rather than the generic person/organisation/date schema of general NER. \autoref{tab:legal-datasets} summarises them with the entity-type distribution of their test mentions.

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:exp:setup}
Embedder, detector and heads are exactly those of the general-domain OPENER (\autoref{sec:method}); the entity embedder is loaded from the Hugging Face Hub and is \emph{not} retrained on legal data. Each test split is capped at $1000$ sentences. Following the general-domain protocol, we report two orthogonal sources of uncertainty. First, the reused embedder's \emph{training} is stochastic: the general-domain study retrains the complete embedder under three seeds ($7$, $42$, $123$; $42$ released), and we repeat the whole legal transfer study with each. Every OPENER cell therefore reports the mean\,$\pm$\,std over the three retraining seeds; the baselines are frozen, deterministic checkpoints, reported as single runs. Second, for a fixed embedder no stage of the pipeline is stochastic at inference, so the remaining uncertainty is the finite test set, quantified with a non-parametric bootstrap ($K{=}1000$): resampling the gold mentions for typing-on-gold (std ${\pm}0.8$--$1.7$ AMI), and resampling the \emph{test sentences}, paired across systems, for the significance test in \autoref{sec:exp:results}. All bootstraps are invariant across the resampling seeds ($42$, $7$, $123$; CIs agree to ${\pm}0.1$ AMI).

For end-to-end runs the detector is a frozen, off-the-shelf GLiNER at threshold $0.3$, fed the target type names; since the detector is a modular, no-cost choice, each OPENER head uses the variant that maximises it (GLiNER-L for OPENER-ZS; GLiNER-M for OPENER-Sup, whose probe is then fit on the detector's own train detections). As baselines we evaluate the same open-world systems as the general-domain benchmark, applied to legal text: GLiNER-S/M/L \citep{zaratiana2024gliner} (DeBERTaV3 \citep{he2023debertav3}), the generative GNER-T5-base \citep{ding2024gner} (T5 \citep{raffel2020t5}), and a 4-bit (NF4 \citep{dettmers2023qlora}) Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct \citep{qwen2025} prompted for entities -- all zero-shot on the target type names. These are \emph{end-to-end} systems (they detect and type jointly), so they are compared only under the end-to-end protocol, never against typing-on-gold numbers. All quality figures are AMI ($\times 100$); German-LER uses its $7$-type coarse schema in the tables, and its $19$-type fine schema as a stress test below.
We also report per-sentence latency (p50) and energy (CodeCarbon), measured with the released embedder on the same $6$\,GB GPU, which thermally throttles under sustained load; per-dataset efficiency cells therefore carry ${\approx}{\pm}15\%$ variance and are read as indicative. Efficiency is deliberately \emph{not} averaged over the retraining seeds: a different seed changes the embedder's weights but not its architecture or per-sentence FLOPs, so any spread across seed re-runs would reflect the machine's thermal state, not the model. OWNER \citep{genest2025owner} runs in batch and exposes no per-sentence call; following the general-domain protocol we therefore report an \emph{amortised} cost from its per-dataset wall-clock time $t$ (model load $+$ mention encoding $+$ clustering): energy $= t\times110$\,W (TDP estimate) and latency $= t/\text{sentences}$.

\subsection{Results}
\label{sec:exp:results}
Keeping the two protocols apart, \autoref{tab:legal-gold} reports typing-on-gold AMI (OPENER heads, perfect detection) and \autoref{tab:legal-main} the end-to-end comparison against the baselines; \autoref{tab:legal-latency}, \autoref{tab:legal-energy} and \autoref{tab:legal-efficiency} give the accompanying latency, energy and a consolidated efficiency summary. Three findings stand out.

\textbf{Label-free typing is strong in English but degrades cross-lingually.} OPENER-ZS is usable on the two English sets -- $47.7$ (E-NER) and $49.1$ (Indian), from English label-name prototypes alone -- but drops to $37.4$ (pt) and $23.8$ (de): its English prototypes align well with English legal mentions, far less with other-language ones (\autoref{tab:legal-gold}). The zero-shot head is where the cross-lingual distribution shift bites hardest. It is also the \emph{seed-sensitive} head: its gold scores vary by ${\pm}3$--$6$ AMI across the three embedder retrainings (the released embedder is in fact a low draw on the English sets), whereas the supervised probe varies by at most ${\pm}1.4$ -- label-name prototypes depend on the fine geometry of the embedding space, which shifts from one contrastive retraining to the next, while a fitted probe absorbs those shifts.

\textbf{Supervised typing transfers well, including across languages, because the separation it learns is geometric, not just statistical.} Fitting a balanced linear probe on the same frozen, general-domain embedder reaches $61.4$ mean typing-on-gold AMI over the four sets -- $70.9$ / $60.8$ on the two English corpora, and still $53.7$ / $60.3$ on Portuguese LeNER-Br and German German-LER -- and is stable across the embedder-retraining seeds (std ${\leq}1.4$ AMI). This is barely below OPENER-Sup's own general-domain score ($62.3$), even though the embedder was never shown a single legal type: a balanced linear probe fits hyperplanes precise enough to pull apart legal types whose vectors sit \emph{close together} (a lawyer from a judge, a statute from a case citation), a fine distinction a single label-name match cannot draw without supervision. The probe adapts to whatever the embedder encodes, so target-label supervision largely absorbs the cross-lingual gap at typing time.

\textbf{End-to-end, detection sets the ceiling, and OPENER-ZS's standing slips relative to the general domain.} On these legal-specific schemas every system clusters in a narrow $\approx 33$--$36$ AMI band (\autoref{tab:legal-main}), because the English detectors recover only a fraction of gold mentions (GLiNER-L offset recall $18.7$--$57.4\%$); with so few mentions detected, no typing head can pull far ahead, and OPENER-ZS's detector fusion essentially reverts to the GLiNER-L labels. Per-dataset winners are specific (OWNER \citep{genest2025owner} on E-NER, GNER-T5 on German), though OWNER degrades most cross-lingually and pays an order-of-magnitude higher inference cost, while Qwen trails throughout and is the slowest. \emph{Averaged over the four sets, OPENER-Sup posts the best end-to-end AMI ($35.9$)} -- narrowly ahead of GLiNER-L ($35.0$) and GLiNER-M ($34.9$), for every retraining seed -- while OPENER-ZS ($33.4$) sits just below OWNER ($33.7$), a marked step down from its \emph{general-domain} standing, where the same fused zero-shot head led every compared system end-to-end ($39.5$ vs GLiNER-L's $38.9$). The margins are small, as expected when detection is the ceiling, yet a \emph{paired} bootstrap on the released model ($K{=}1000$) shows OPENER-Sup's end-to-end lead is nonetheless \textbf{statistically significant} ($+1.0$ AMI over both GLiNER-M and GLiNER-L, $95\%$ CI $[+0.4,+1.7]$ and $[+0.1,+1.9]$); OPENER-ZS's fusion, by contrast, sits significantly \emph{below} GLiNER-L ($-1.3$, $[-1.8,-0.8]$), largely deferring to the detector's labels. Detection remains the ceiling, but OPENER-Sup's typing head is not the weak link -- consistent with the typing-on-gold results, where it leads by a wide margin.

\subsection{Analysis}
\label{sec:exp:analysis}
Two distribution shifts act together here, and the results separate them cleanly. On English legal text (E-NER), where only the \emph{domain} shifts, transfer is strong: OPENER-ZS reaches a usable $47.7$ gold AMI with no target labels at all, and OPENER-Sup $70.9$, confirming that the general-domain contrastive embedder already places legal entity types close enough together to be separated, whether by a label-name prototype or, far more precisely, by a supervised hyperplane. Adding a cross-lingual shift (Portuguese, German) widens the gap: the label-free head drops to $24$--$37$ as its English prototypes no longer match target-language mentions, whereas the supervised probe stays high ($53.7$/$60.3$) because it re-fits on target labels.

The specialised-schema stress test isolates fineness of separation from language. On German-LER's fine $19$-type schema, OPENER-ZS falls further to $22.7_{\pm2.1}$ gold AMI, as fine German type codes often have no faithful English label-name prototype. OPENER-Sup, in contrast, still reaches $61.5_{\pm0.7}$, \emph{barely below its $7$-type coarse score} ($60.3$): the embedder separates even fine legal distinctions once a probe names them, regardless of how close their embeddings sit. The zero-shot weakness is therefore not the embedding geometry, but the missing supervised signal to exploit it.

The binding constraint end-to-end is detection, not typing: GLiNER-L's offset recall falls from $57.4\%$ (en) to $18.7\%$ (de), so the end-to-end scores compress to a ${\approx}33$--$36$ band regardless of the typing head, within which OPENER-Sup nonetheless posts the best average ($35.9$) by re-typing the detected mentions with target-label supervision. This localises where domain adaptation would pay off first: a light, in-language adaptation of the \emph{detector} (or a multilingual one) would lift the ceiling far more than a better typing head. Within our frugal, retraining-free budget, OPENER-Sup is therefore the practical default for legal and other applied deployments that can supply even a modest labelled sample, while OPENER-ZS remains a useful no-label fallback only when the target language stays close to the embedder's own (English, general) training distribution.

\textbf{Fusion weight $\beta$.} OPENER-ZS's detector-fusion weight $\beta$ (fixed at $0.05$ throughout, inherited from the general-domain study and never tuned on the legal test sets) balances the label-name centroids against the detector's own zero-shot labels. Sweeping it post-hoc on the released model -- a pure inference hyperparameter, leaving the embedder untouched -- tells the same story: at $\beta{=}0$ the head is a pure centroid classifier ($30.5$ average AMI), and as $\beta$ grows it interpolates smoothly up to the GLiNER-L detector's own labels ($35.0$), a plateau it already reaches by $\beta{\approx}0.2$. The label-free head can therefore do no better end-to-end than the detector it fuses with -- one more sign that detection, not typing, is the binding constraint.
