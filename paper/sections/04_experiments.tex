\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Datasets}
\label{sec:exp:datasets}
We evaluate on three public legal NER datasets that, together, separate two kinds of distribution shift. \textbf{E-NER} \citep{au2022ener} -- annotated U.S.\ SEC/EDGAR filings -- is in \emph{English}, so it isolates a \emph{pure domain shift} from the general-domain data the embedder was trained on. \textbf{LeNER-Br} \citep{luzdearaujo2018lenerbr} (Brazilian court decisions, Portuguese) and \textbf{German-LER} \citep{leitner2020germanler} (German federal court decisions) add a \emph{cross-lingual} shift on top of the domain shift, stressing an English-centric embedder well outside its training distribution. \autoref{tab:legal-datasets} summarises them with the entity-type distribution of their test mentions; all three exhibit a strongly skewed, long-tailed inventory typical of legal text (one or two dominant types and several rare, schema-specific ones).

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:exp:setup}
Embedder, detector and heads are exactly those of the general-domain OPENER (\autoref{sec:method}); the entity embedder is loaded from the Hugging Face Hub and is \emph{not} retrained on legal data. Single fixed seed; each test split capped at $1000$ sentences. % TODO[venue]: multi-seed if space/time allow.
For end-to-end runs the detector is GLiNER-L at threshold $0.3$, fed the target type names. As a reference point we also report \textbf{GLiNER-L standalone}: the same detector keeping its \emph{own} zero-shot type predictions (no OPENER typing head), which is the natural open-world baseline on the detected spans. All quality figures are AMI ($\times 100$); the German coarse schema ($7$ types) is used in \autoref{tab:legal-main}, and the $19$-type fine schema is discussed as a stress test below.

\subsection{Results}
\label{sec:exp:results}
\autoref{tab:legal-main} reports AMI for OPENER-Sup and OPENER-ZS in both protocols, and \autoref{tab:legal-frugal} the accompanying frugality. Three findings stand out.

\textbf{Supervised typing transfers well, including across languages.} Fitting a balanced linear probe on the frozen, general-domain embedder reaches $60.8$ mean typing-on-gold AMI -- $70.0$ on the English E-NER (a pure domain shift), and still $53.3$ / $59.1$ on Portuguese LeNER-Br and German German-LER despite the English-centric embedder (\autoref{fig:legal-gold}). The probe adapts to whatever the embedder encodes, so target-label supervision largely absorbs the cross-lingual gap at typing time.

\textbf{Label-free typing holds under domain shift but degrades cross-lingually.} OPENER-ZS reaches $44.0$ gold AMI on English E-NER, but only $31.6$ (pt) and $22.6$ (de): its English label-name prototypes align well with English legal mentions, far less with Portuguese/German ones. The zero-shot head is where the cross-lingual distribution shift bites hardest.

\textbf{End-to-end quality is capped by the detector.} All three systems collapse to $\approx 34$--$35$ end-to-end, because the English GLiNER-L recovers only $57.4\%$ of gold mentions on E-NER, $44.0\%$ on LeNER-Br and $18.7\%$ on German-LER (offset recall). With so few mentions detected in the non-English sets, the typing head has little left to differentiate, and OPENER-ZS's detector fusion essentially reverts to the GLiNER-L labels ($35.0$).

\subsection{Analysis}
\label{sec:exp:analysis}
Two distribution shifts act together here, and the results separate them cleanly. On \textbf{English} legal text (E-NER), where only the \emph{domain} shifts, transfer is strong: OPENER-Sup reaches $70.0$ gold AMI and OPENER-ZS a usable $44.0$ with no target labels at all, confirming that the general-domain contrastive embedder already places legal entity types (business, court, government body, legislation) in largely separable regions. Adding a \textbf{cross-lingual} shift (Portuguese, German) widens the gap between the two heads: the supervised probe stays high ($53.3$/$59.1$) because it re-fits on target labels, whereas the label-free head drops to $22$--$32$ because its English prototypes and the English embedder no longer match the target-language mentions.

The \textbf{specialised-schema} stress test points the same way. On German-LER's fine $19$-type schema, OPENER-Sup still reaches $62.0$ gold AMI -- the embedder separates even fine legal distinctions once a probe names them -- but OPENER-ZS falls to $22.1$, as many fine German type codes have no faithful English label-name prototype.

The binding constraint end-to-end is \textbf{detection}, not typing: GLiNER-L's offset recall falls from $57.4\%$ (en) to $18.7\%$ (de), so all end-to-end scores compress to ${\approx}34$--$35$ regardless of the typing head. This localises where domain adaptation would pay off first: a light, in-language adaptation of the \emph{detector} (or a multilingual one) would lift the ceiling far more than a better typing head. Within our frugal, retraining-free budget, OPENER-Sup is the safe operating point across languages, while OPENER-ZS is attractive only when the target language is close to the embedder's (English) training distribution.
