\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Datasets}
\label{sec:exp:datasets}
We evaluate on four public legal NER datasets that, together, separate two kinds of distribution shift. \emph{Two are in English}, isolating a \emph{pure domain shift} from the general-domain data the embedder was trained on: \textbf{E-NER} \citep{au2022ener} (annotated U.S.\ SEC/EDGAR filings, $7$ types) and \textbf{Indian-Legal} \citep{kalamkar2022indianlegal} (Indian court judgments, $14$ fine-grained legal types). \textbf{LeNER-Br} \citep{luzdearaujo2018lenerbr} (Brazilian court decisions, Portuguese) and \textbf{German-LER} \citep{leitner2020germanler} (German federal court decisions) add a \emph{cross-lingual} shift on top of the domain shift, stressing an English-centric embedder well outside its training distribution. \autoref{tab:legal-datasets} summarises them with the entity-type distribution of their test mentions.

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:exp:setup}
Embedder, detector and heads are exactly those of the general-domain OPENER (\autoref{sec:method}); the entity embedder is loaded from the Hugging Face Hub and is \emph{not} retrained on legal data. Single fixed seed; each test split capped at $1000$ sentences. Since the reused embedder is frozen and no stage of the pipeline is stochastic at inference, the quality scores are deterministic (identical across re-runs and across seeds); following the general-domain protocol, we therefore quantify their sampling uncertainty with a test-set bootstrap ($K{=}1000$ resamples of the gold mentions) rather than a multi-seed \emph{retraining} study, reporting $95\%$ confidence intervals in \autoref{tab:legal-bootstrap}.

For end-to-end runs the detector is GLiNER-L at threshold $0.3$, fed the target type names. As \textbf{baselines} we evaluate the same open-world systems as the general-domain benchmark, applied to legal text: GLiNER-S/M/L \cite{zaratiana2024gliner}, the generative GNER-T5-base \cite{ding2024gner}, and a 4-bit Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct \cite{qwen2025} prompted for entities -- all zero-shot on the target type names. These are \emph{end-to-end} systems (they detect and type jointly), so they are compared only under the end-to-end protocol, never against typing-on-gold numbers. All quality figures are AMI ($\times 100$); German-LER uses its $7$-type coarse schema in the tables, and its $19$-type fine schema as a stress test below.

\subsection{Results}
\label{sec:exp:results}
Keeping the two protocols apart, \autoref{tab:legal-gold} reports typing-on-gold AMI (OPENER heads, perfect detection) and \autoref{tab:legal-main} the end-to-end comparison against the baselines; \autoref{tab:legal-frugal} gives the accompanying frugality. Three findings stand out.

\textbf{Supervised typing transfers well, including across languages.} Fitting a balanced linear probe on the frozen, general-domain embedder reaches $61.1$ mean typing-on-gold AMI over the four sets -- $70.0$ on E-NER and $61.9$ on the English Indian court corpus, and still $53.3$ / $59.1$ on Portuguese LeNER-Br and German German-LER despite the English-centric embedder (\autoref{fig:legal-gold}). The probe adapts to whatever the embedder encodes, so target-label supervision largely absorbs the cross-lingual gap at typing time.

\textbf{Label-free typing is strong in English but degrades cross-lingually.} OPENER-ZS is best on the two English sets -- $44.0$ on E-NER and its overall best $46.3$ on the Indian court corpus, whose type names (\emph{court}, \emph{judge}, \emph{statute}, \dots) are highly nameable -- but drops to $31.6$ (pt) and $22.6$ (de): its English label-name prototypes align well with English legal mentions, far less with Portuguese/German ones. The zero-shot head is where the cross-lingual distribution shift bites hardest.

\textbf{End-to-end, detection is the bottleneck, not typing.} Most systems compress into a narrow $\approx 33$--$35$ AMI band (\autoref{tab:legal-main}) -- GLiNER-S/M/L, OWNER, OPENER-ZS and OPENER-Sup all average $33.7$--$35.0$ -- because the English detectors recover only a fraction of gold mentions (GLiNER-L offset recall: $57.4\%$ on E-NER, $44.5\%$ on Indian-Legal, $44.0\%$ on LeNER-Br, $18.7\%$ on German-LER); with so few mentions detected, no typing head can pull far ahead, and OPENER-ZS's detector fusion essentially reverts to the GLiNER-L labels. Two systems break out on a single dataset: GNER-T5 on German ($39.2$, its generative pretraining detects better there) and OWNER \cite{genest2025owner} -- the closest prior work -- on E-NER ($38.4$). OWNER, however, degrades most under the cross-lingual shift ($28.4$ on German) and pays an order-of-magnitude higher inference cost, re-encoding and clustering every mention at test time, whereas OPENER keeps a frugal, fixed-cost typing head. The 4-bit Qwen2.5-1.5B trails every dedicated system ($23.1$ mean) while being the slowest. No system dominates end-to-end -- detection, not typing, is the ceiling -- and OPENER-Sup/ZS sit in the leading band (both $33.7$--$33.8$ average) at a fraction of the per-sentence cost.

The three-axis picture (\autoref{tab:legal-frugal}, \autoref{fig:legal-efficiency}) mirrors the general-domain study: OPENER types a sentence end-to-end in ${\approx}120$\,ms for ${\approx}2.1$\,Wh -- on par with the GLiNER family and OWNER, and \emph{one to two orders of magnitude} below the generative GNER-T5 ($5.7$\,s, $67$\,Wh) and Qwen ($4.9$\,s, $16$\,Wh). Zero-shot typing on gold is cheaper still ($0.5$\,Wh, $32$\,ms), as it embeds no training split. OPENER thus keeps the leading accuracy band at a small, fixed inference cost, which is the operating point that makes it deployable on modest hardware.

\subsection{Analysis}
\label{sec:exp:analysis}
Two distribution shifts act together here, and the results separate them cleanly. On \textbf{English} legal text (E-NER), where only the \emph{domain} shifts, transfer is strong: OPENER-Sup reaches $70.0$ gold AMI and OPENER-ZS a usable $44.0$ with no target labels at all, confirming that the general-domain contrastive embedder already places legal entity types (business, court, government body, legislation) in largely separable regions. Adding a \textbf{cross-lingual} shift (Portuguese, German) widens the gap between the two heads: the supervised probe stays high ($53.3$/$59.1$) because it re-fits on target labels, whereas the label-free head drops to $22$--$32$ because its English prototypes and the English embedder no longer match the target-language mentions.

The \textbf{specialised-schema} stress test points the same way. On German-LER's fine $19$-type schema, OPENER-Sup still reaches $62.0$ gold AMI -- the embedder separates even fine legal distinctions once a probe names them -- but OPENER-ZS falls to $22.1$, as many fine German type codes have no faithful English label-name prototype.

The binding constraint end-to-end is \textbf{detection}, not typing: GLiNER-L's offset recall falls from $57.4\%$ (en) to $18.7\%$ (de), so all end-to-end scores compress to ${\approx}34$--$35$ regardless of the typing head. This localises where domain adaptation would pay off first: a light, in-language adaptation of the \emph{detector} (or a multilingual one) would lift the ceiling far more than a better typing head. Within our frugal, retraining-free budget, OPENER-Sup is the safe operating point across languages, while OPENER-ZS is attractive only when the target language is close to the embedder's (English) training distribution.
