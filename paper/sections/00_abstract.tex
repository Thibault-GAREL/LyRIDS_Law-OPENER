Open-world named entity recognition (NER) must assign entity types that are not fixed at training time, a setting that is pervasive in the legal domain: new statutes, parties, instruments, courts and jurisdictions appear continually, and re-annotating and retraining for each is costly.
We ask whether \textbf{OPENER}, a frugal open-world NER pipeline assembled entirely from off-the-shelf pretrained components and trained only on general-domain data, \emph{transfers} to legal NER \emph{without any legal-domain retraining}.
We reuse its contrastively fine-tuned, Matryoshka-truncatable entity embedder unchanged (released on the Hugging Face Hub) behind a frozen open-world detector, and evaluate two lightweight typing heads on \textbf{six legal NER sets across four languages} -- three English (SEC filings, Indian court judgments, EUR-Lex legislation), plus French, Portuguese and German%
: \textbf{OPENER-Sup}, a balanced linear probe fit on the target labels, and \textbf{OPENER-ZS}, a label-free head combining label-name prototypes, a transductive refinement and a fusion with the detector's own zero-shot predictions.
Following the general-domain protocol, we report quality (Adjusted Mutual Information) jointly with inference latency and energy.
Transfer holds strongly under a pure (English) domain shift -- on the three English sets OPENER-Sup reaches up to $70.0$ AMI on gold mentions and the label-free OPENER-ZS up to $56.5$ -- and the supervised probe remains robust across languages ($58.1$ mean gold AMI), at only $0.5$--$1.7$\,Wh per run. It degrades under the added cross-lingual shift (French, Portuguese, German), where the label-free head drops to $22$--$36$; a controlled English$\to$French ablation shows this cost falls on the whole English-centric pipeline -- detector included -- not on OPENER's typing head.
Our results characterise how far a general-purpose, energy-frugal open-world pipeline carries over to specialised legal schemas, and show that in-language \emph{detection} is where domain adaptation would help first.
