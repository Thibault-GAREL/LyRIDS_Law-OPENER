Open-world named entity recognition (NER) must assign entity types that are not fixed at training time, a setting that is pervasive in the legal domain: new statutes, parties, instruments, courts and jurisdictions appear continually, and re-annotating and retraining for each is costly.
We ask whether \textbf{OPENER}, a frugal open-world NER pipeline assembled entirely from off-the-shelf pretrained components and trained only on general-domain data, \emph{transfers} to legal NER \emph{without any legal-domain retraining}.
We reuse its contrastively fine-tuned, Matryoshka-truncatable entity embedder unchanged (released on the Hugging Face Hub) behind a frozen open-world detector, and evaluate two lightweight typing heads on \textbf{$N$ legal NER datasets} (\textsc{languages})%
: \textbf{OPENER-Sup}, a balanced linear probe fit on the target labels, and \textbf{OPENER-ZS}, a label-free head combining label-name prototypes, a transductive refinement and a fusion with the detector's own zero-shot predictions.
Following the general-domain protocol, we report quality (Adjusted Mutual Information) jointly with inference latency and energy.
