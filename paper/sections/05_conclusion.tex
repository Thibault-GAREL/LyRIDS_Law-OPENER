\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We studied whether \textbf{OPENER}, a frugal open-world NER pipeline assembled from off-the-shelf pretrained parts and trained only on general-domain data, transfers to legal NER without any legal-domain retraining. Reusing its entity embedder unchanged, we evaluated a supervised linear probe (OPENER-Sup) and a label-free head (OPENER-ZS: prototypes, transductive refinement, detector fusion) on three legal datasets in English, Portuguese and German along quality, latency and energy.
Transfer holds strongly under a pure (English) domain shift -- $70.0$ gold AMI supervised, $44.0$ label-free -- and the supervised probe stays robust across languages ($60.8$ mean gold AMI) at $0.5$--$1.7$\,Wh per run, confirming that a general-domain contrastive embedder already separates legal entity types. Under the added cross-lingual shift the label-free head weakens ($22$--$32$ AMI), and end-to-end every system is capped near $34$--$35$ AMI by the English detector's recall (down to $18.7\%$ on German). The bottleneck is therefore \emph{detection}, not typing: light in-language adaptation of the detector -- rather than of the embedder -- is the natural next step, while keeping the frugal, decoupled detect-then-type design intact.

\paragraph{Limitations} A single fixed seed and test splits capped at $1000$ sentences (inference is deterministic, so quality scores carry no run-to-run noise). More fundamentally, both the embedder and the detector are English-centric and reused without any legal- or language-domain adaptation; the detector's low cross-lingual recall (e.g.\ $18.7\%$ on German) caps end-to-end quality and is the first thing a follow-up should address.
