\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We studied whether \textbf{OPENER}, a frugal open-world NER pipeline assembled from off-the-shelf pretrained parts and trained only on general-domain data, transfers to legal NER without any legal-domain retraining. Reusing its entity embedder unchanged, we evaluated a supervised linear probe (OPENER-Sup) and a label-free head (OPENER-ZS: prototypes, transductive refinement, detector fusion) on four legal-specific NER sets across three languages -- two English, plus Portuguese and German -- along quality, latency and energy, against the same open-world baselines as the general-domain benchmark.
Transfer holds strongly under a pure (English) domain shift -- $70.9$ gold AMI supervised, $47.7$ label-free, averaged over three embedder-retraining seeds -- and the supervised probe stays robust across languages ($61.4$ mean gold AMI) at $0.5$--$1.7$\,Wh per run, confirming that a general-domain contrastive embedder already separates legal entity types. Under the added cross-lingual shift the label-free head weakens ($24$--$37$ AMI), and end-to-end every system compresses into a narrow $33$--$36$ AMI band set by the English detector's recall (down to $18.7\%$ on German); within it OPENER-Sup still posts the best average ($35.9$), one to two orders of magnitude cheaper than the generative baselines. Echoing the general-domain study, the label-free OPENER-ZS behaves like the other zero-shot systems -- its end-to-end quality is bounded by mention detection and by what the frozen, general-domain embedder was trained on -- whereas the supervised OPENER-Sup comes out first because its balanced probe captures finer type distinctions than the competing systems (a paired bootstrap makes this end-to-end lead statistically significant). The bottleneck is therefore \emph{detection}, not typing: light in-language adaptation of the detector -- rather than of the embedder -- is the natural next step, while keeping the frugal, decoupled detect-then-type design intact.

\paragraph{Limitations} Test splits are capped at $1000$ sentences. OPENER scores are means over three embedder-retraining seeds: the supervised and end-to-end scores are stable (std ${\leq}1.4$ AMI), but the label-free prototypes vary by up to ${\pm}6$ AMI across retrainings, so single-seed zero-shot numbers should be read with care. Inference is deterministic for a fixed embedder, and test-set bootstraps on the released model confirm both the typing-on-gold scores (std ${\pm}0.8$--$1.7$ AMI) and the significance of OPENER-Sup's end-to-end lead, stable across resampling seeds $42/7/123$. More fundamentally, both the embedder and the detector are English-centric and reused without any legal- or language-domain adaptation; the detector's low cross-lingual recall (e.g.\ $18.7\%$ on German) caps end-to-end quality and is the first thing a follow-up should address.
